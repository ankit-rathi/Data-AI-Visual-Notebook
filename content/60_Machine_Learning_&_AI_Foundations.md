From first principles, organizations make decisions under uncertainty. The challenge is not eliminating uncertainty, but allocating resources in ways that improve expected outcomes. Machine learning is a tool for that purpose. It uses historical data to estimate probabilities or recommend actions, effectively redistributing uncertainty toward better expected value. A fraud model, for example, does not guarantee which transactions are fraudulent; it estimates risk so capital and attention can be allocated more intelligently.

Learning can take different forms. In supervised learning, models learn from labeled examples — such as past customers marked “churned” or “retained” — to predict future outcomes. In unsupervised learning, there are no labels; the system identifies structure, such as grouping customers into segments based on behavior. The choice depends on whether the objective is prediction or pattern discovery.

Training and inference reflect two distinct phases. Training is the computationally intensive process of learning patterns from data. Inference is the real-time or batch application of those patterns to new cases, such as scoring a loan application in milliseconds. Each phase has different performance and reliability requirements.

A central risk is overfitting — when a model captures noise in historical data rather than underlying signal. Such a model performs well on past data but fails on new inputs. Generalization is the goal: identifying patterns that hold beyond the training sample. Validation techniques, held-out data, and regularization help guard against false confidence.

Evaluation must align with decision impact. High statistical accuracy is meaningless if it does not improve profit, reduce risk, or enhance customer value. Monitoring is equally critical because environments change. Data drift (input shifts) and concept drift (changing relationships) cause model decay, requiring retraining and oversight.

Neural networks extend modeling capacity by learning layered transformations capable of capturing complex relationships, especially in images or text. Embeddings compress high-dimensional inputs, like words, into dense vectors that encode similarity. Large language models predict text token by token, enabling generation and reasoning based on statistical patterns. Retrieval-augmented generation improves reliability by grounding outputs in external documents.

Yet ML is not always appropriate. Simple rules may outperform complex models when data is sparse or interpretability is critical. Ultimately, machine learning is valuable only when it improves decision quality under uncertainty while preserving trust, cost discipline, and strategic clarity.
