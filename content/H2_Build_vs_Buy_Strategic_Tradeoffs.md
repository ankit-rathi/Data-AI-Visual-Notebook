## Build vs Buy ‚Äî Strategic Tradeoffs

In the previous topic, we focused on measuring ROI. We clarified that Data and AI initiatives must justify their cost through measurable business impact.

Once ROI discipline is established, a strategic question naturally follows:

Should we build the capability ourselves, or should we buy it?

This is not a technical decision. It is a capital allocation decision.

Building offers control. It allows customization, deeper integration, and long-term ownership of intellectual property. Internal teams develop expertise, and the organization becomes less dependent on external vendors.

But building is expensive. It requires talent, time, management attention, and ongoing maintenance. Delays are common. Opportunity cost accumulates while systems are being developed.

Buying offers speed. Proven solutions can be deployed faster, often with lower initial risk. Vendors spread development costs across many customers, which can reduce per-unit expense.

But buying introduces dependency. Roadmaps are influenced by external priorities. Customization may be limited. Long-term costs can grow through licensing and integration complexity.

The mistake organizations make is framing this as a binary technical preference. The real lens is strategic leverage.

Build when the capability creates differentiated advantage, when it directly influences core value creation, or when control over data and logic is essential.

Buy when the capability is standardized, when speed matters more than customization, or when the function does not define competitive edge.

The tradeoff is between control and speed, ownership and flexibility, long-term leverage and short-term efficiency.

ROI discipline helps evaluate this choice. The question becomes: which path produces greater net value over time, accounting for total cost, risk, and strategic positioning?

Build vs buy is not about pride or preference. It is about aligning capability investment with competitive strategy.

And once a path is chosen, another constraint emerges:

Are incentives aligned across the people who must make it succeed?

That is the next layer of strategic design.
