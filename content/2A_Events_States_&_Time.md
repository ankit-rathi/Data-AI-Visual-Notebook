## **A. Events, States & Time**

**Why:** Decisions require understanding **how reality changes**, not just static snapshots. Without temporal context, you miss causality.

**What:** Events are **things that happen**, states are **conditions at a moment**, and time orders them.

**How:** Capture sequences and transitions to preserve causality. For example, tracking user actions over time helps infer behavior patterns, not just totals.

---

From first principles, reality unfolds over time. Nothing meaningful exists as a permanent snapshot; systems evolve through sequences of change. If we observe only static states — balances, totals, averages — we lose the causal story of how those states emerged. Decisions made without temporal structure risk misinterpreting correlation as cause.

An **event** is something that happens: a purchase, a login, a price change, a payment default. A **state** is the condition of a system at a specific moment: account balance, inventory level, customer status. Time orders events and connects them into sequences, allowing us to understand transitions between states.

Consider a bank account. A snapshot shows a balance of ₹10,000. That state alone tells us nothing about behavior. Did the balance grow steadily through savings? Was there a recent large deposit? Is the account typically near zero but temporarily inflated? Only by examining the sequence of deposits and withdrawals — the events — can we understand patterns such as income stability or spending volatility.

Causality depends on order. If a marketing email is sent (event) and purchases increase afterward (subsequent events), there may be a causal link. But if purchases rose before the email, the interpretation changes entirely. Without time ordering, cause and effect blur.

In user analytics, aggregate metrics like “monthly active users” are states. They describe system condition but hide behavioral pathways. Tracking event sequences — sign-up → onboarding completion → feature usage → purchase — reveals friction points and conversion drivers. The difference is between knowing *how many* users exist and knowing *how they move* through the system.

Temporal data also enables transition analysis. A credit portfolio’s default rate is a state-level metric. But observing transitions — current → 30 days past due → 60 days past due → default — uncovers early warning signals. Interventions become possible because change is visible before final outcomes occur.

From a systems perspective, decisions alter future states by triggering events. Understanding sequences allows organizations to estimate expected transitions and intervene at leverage points.

Static snapshots summarize. Temporal sequences explain.

Strong decision systems therefore preserve event logs, timestamps, and state transitions. They analyze flows, not just totals. By respecting time as a structural dimension, organizations maintain causal clarity and reduce the risk of drawing conclusions from incomplete pictures of reality.
