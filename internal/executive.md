# 25-Minute Executive Pitch

## “From Data to Decisions: Installing Value Accountability in Our Data Investments”

---

# 0–3 Minutes — Open With the Institutional Gap

Start direct.

> “We rigorously track what we spend on data projects.
> We do not rigorously track what economic value they generate.”

Then ground it in reality:

* Every project has budget approval.
* Few have quantified expected value.
* Post-launch impact discussions are anecdotal.
* This is not a team issue — it’s systemic.

Make it uncomfortable but factual.

Then add:

> “In a regulated bank, this is a capital allocation blind spot.”

Now they are listening.

---

# 3–8 Minutes — The Economic Lens (Simple, Executive-Level)

Reframe the issue.

1. A decision = capital committed under uncertainty.
2. Value emerges only if a decision improves.
3. Every data project must move one of three levers:

   * Revenue
   * Cost
   * Risk
4. Without baseline + counterfactual, attribution is guesswork.

Then say:

> “We are very good at building solutions.
> We are inconsistent at measuring decision uplift.”

Pause.

That’s the core insight.

---

# 8–15 Minutes — Internal Case Studies (This Is Your Power Section)

## Case Study 1 — Strategic Dashboard Automation

Before:

* Manual Excel
* Quarterly review
* Error-prone, not scalable

After:

* Automated ingestion
* Golden sources
* Lower operational risk

Now quantify pathways:

Economic levers:

* Manual cost reduction
* Reduced reporting error probability
* Potential increase in redundancy detection

Explain honestly:

> “We delivered successfully.
> But we did not structure impact measurement upfront.
> Post-delivery attribution became difficult.”

This shows intellectual maturity.

---

## Case Study 2 — DSAR Automation (GDPR Compliance)

Before:

* Manual handling
* 30–40 requests/day
* 30-day SLA
* High human-error exposure

After:

* Automated, traceable workflow
* Scalable ingestion
* SLA reliability

Now quantify:

~12,000 compliance events per year.

Economic pathways:

* Reduced manual cost per request
* Reduced probability of SLA breach
* Reduced expected regulatory exposure
* Audit defensibility

Say this clearly:

> “This was not an IT automation project.
> It was a regulatory risk mitigation project.”

Now they see economic framing.

---

# 15–20 Minutes — The Structural Problem

Summarize what both cases reveal:

* Decision was not formally defined upfront.
* Baseline not numerically documented.
* Expected value not estimated pre-build.
* No structured post-launch attribution review.

Therefore:

> We rely on perceived value rather than quantified value.

That is risky in a capital-constrained, regulated environment.

Now introduce the solution at high level:

---

# 20–23 Minutes — The Proposed Solution

Not a training course.

Frame it as:

> “A Decision-Value Accountability Program.”

Core elements:

1. Pre-build Decision Impact Charter
2. Baseline quantification discipline
3. Expected value estimation before funding
4. Post-launch 6-month attribution review
5. Portfolio-level visibility of data ROI

Clarify:

This does NOT slow delivery.
It improves capital discipline.

This is not bureaucracy.
It is economic hygiene.

---

# 23–25 Minutes — The Ask

Be precise.

Propose:

* Pilot the program with 2–3 live projects.
* Run internal workshop across data + business leads.
* Implement lightweight Decision Impact Charter template.
* Review outcomes after 1 quarter.

Close with:

> “We don’t need more dashboards.
> We need measurable decision impact.”

---

# Tone Guidance (Critical)

Be calm.
Be analytical.
Avoid evangelism.

You are not selling education.
You are proposing risk-adjusted capital discipline.

---

# What Executives Should Leave With

1. We lack structured ROI attribution in data.
2. This is fixable.
3. We have internal proof points.
4. This can strengthen governance and funding decisions.

---

# Optional: Strong Closing Line

> “If we cannot estimate expected value before building,
> we are allocating capital on belief, not economics.”

That line lands.
